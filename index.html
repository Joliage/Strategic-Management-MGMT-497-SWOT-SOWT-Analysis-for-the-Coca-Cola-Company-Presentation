Chapter 6 · The big idea

Strategy analysis & choice

This stage turns the homework of earlier chapters — the audits, the matrices — into action. It generates feasible alternative strategies, evaluates them, and selects the ones the firm should actually pursue.

  • It is subjective decisions built on objective information — judgement guided by numbers, not guesswork.
  • Strategies "don't come out of the blue" — they grow from the firm's vision, mission, audits, and past wins.
  • Unless the EFE/CPM/IFE scores are very low, good alternatives are usually incremental moves toward a better position.
Rudin's Law

"When a crisis forces choosing among alternatives, most people choose the worst possible one."

A structured framework exists precisely to keep choice from collapsing into panic or politics.

Stage 1

The input stage — quantifying subjectivity

Three matrices summarise the basic information needed to formulate strategy. They force strategists to put numbers on judgement before generating alternatives.

EFE
External Factor Evaluation — opportunities & threats outside the firm
IFE
Internal Factor Evaluation — the firm's own strengths & weaknesses
CPM
Competitive Profile Matrix — the firm scored against its rivals
Why it matters A rating of 3 is 50% larger than a 2 — small weighting and rating choices compound into big strategic differences. This is why we lock a single shared IFE/EFE for Part 1.
Refresher

Reading an EFE / IFE matrix

The four columns

  • Factor — a specific, quantified external or internal item.
  • Weight (0–1) — how important that factor is to success. All weights sum to 1.00.
  • Rating (1–4) — how well the firm currently responds (EFE) or how strong it is (IFE).
  • Weighted score — weight × rating.

The total score

  • Add every weighted score → a total between 1.0 and 4.0.
  • 2.5 is the average mark.
  • Above 2.5 → the firm responds well to its situation.
  • Below 2.5 → the firm is struggling to respond.
Coca-Cola → EFE 3.12 and IFE 2.88 — both above the 2.5 line.
Refresher · the 1–4 scale

What the ratings say

EFE — response to external factors

  • 4 = superior response   3 = above average
  • 2 = average   1 = poor response
  • Applies to both opportunities and threats.

IFE — internal capability

  • 4 = major strength   3 = minor strength
  • 2 = minor weakness   1 = major weakness
  • Strengths must rate 3–4; weaknesses must rate 1–2.
AQCD test Good factors are Actionable, Quantitative, Comparative, and Divisional. Notice every Coca-Cola factor carries a number, percent, or dollar figure.

Pairs S·W·O·T into SO/WO/ST/WT strategies. The focus of this assignment.

SPACE

Aggressive, conservative, defensive, or competitive posture.

BCG

Stars, cash cows, question marks, dogs across divisions.

Nine-cell grid from IFE × EFE → grow / hold / harvest.

The matching tool of record

The SWOT matrix

SWOT is the most widely used strategy tool because it is simple and sparks discussion. Its job is to generate a list of feasible strategies — not to pick the winner.

  • A nine-cell grid: four factor cells (S, W, O, T), four strategy cells (SO, WO, ST, WT), and one cell always left blank.
  • You build the factor cells first, then match across them to write strategies.
  • Each strategy carries a notation such as S1, O2 showing exactly which factors were combined.

The four quadrants

SO · WO · ST · WT

SO · Offensive

Use internal strengths to take advantage of external opportunities. The ideal position — play to what you're great at.

WO · Improving

Overcome internal weaknesses by seizing external opportunities — e.g. build a missing capability the market now rewards.

ST · Defending

Use internal strengths to avoid or reduce external threats — e.g. a strong legal team fighting patent infringers.

WT · Survival

Defensive moves that reduce internal weakness while avoiding external threats — retrench, divest, or exit.

The assignment's framing

Offense and defense — at the same time

OFFENSE

SO & ST

Built on strengths. SO chases growth; ST protects the lead. These are how Coca-Cola presses its advantages.

DEFENSE

WO & WT

Built on closing weaknesses. WO upgrades the firm; WT shores up survival. These are how Coca-Cola covers its gaps.

"A company must do both at the same time." — and that is exactly what an 8-strategy SWOT delivers.
Method · eight steps

Building the matrix, step by step

  • 1. List key internal strengths.
  • 2. List key internal weaknesses.
  • 3. List key external opportunities.
  • 4. List key external threats.
  • 5. Match S × O → write SO strategies.
  • 6. Match W × O → write WO strategies.
  • 7. Match S × T → write ST strategies.
  • 8. Match W × T → write WT strategies.
Rule of thumb Aim for ~4 strategies per quadrant (≈16 total) to cover the whole business. Assignment 12 requires at least two per quadrant — eight minimum.
The golden rule

Specificity is everything

The test: "Could I estimate the cost or savings of this strategy?" If not, it's too vague. Whenever you'd write expand, increase, grow, more, reduce — attach a number.

✗ Too vague

  • "Forward integration" (that's a strategy type, not a strategy)
  • "Do more R&D"
  • "Expand globally"
  • "Add repair staff"

✓ Specific & costable

  • "Sign 5-year exclusive fountain contracts with the top 30 US chains"
  • "Invest $50M in 3 new Honest Tea flavors"
  • "Build 2 bottling plants in India & Vietnam (~$80M)"
  • "Open 200 new stores at 10K sq ft each"

Assignment 12 · Part 3

The 11 strategy types

Integration

  • Forward integration — control distributors/retailers
  • Backward integration — control suppliers
  • Horizontal integration — acquire competitors

Intensive

  • Market penetration — more share, current products/markets
  • Market development — new geographic markets
  • Product development — new/improved products

Diversification

  • Related diversification — new, connected businesses
  • Unrelated diversification — new, unconnected businesses

Defensive

  • Retrenchment — regroup via cost/asset cuts
  • Divestiture — sell a division or segment
  • Liquidation — sell all assets, close down

Matching tool · Figure 6-4

The SPACE matrix

Two axes, four quadrants — telling a firm whether to be aggressive, conservative, defensive, or competitive.

  • FP — Financial Position (internal, +1 to +7)
  • CP — Competitive Position (internal, −1 to −7)
  • SP — Stability Position (external, −1 to −7)
  • IP — Industry Position (external, +1 to +7)
X = CP + IP  ·  Y = FP + SP. The vector's quadrant is the recommendation.

The BCG matrix

Portfolio analysis on two axes: relative market share (x) and industry growth rate (y). Each circle is a division; its size is revenue, its slice is profit.

  • Stars — high share, high growth → invest.
  • Cash Cows — high share, low growth → milk.
  • Question Marks — low share, high growth → decide.
  • Dogs — low share, low growth → trim / divest.

The Internal-External (IE) matrix

A nine-cell grid plotting IFE total (x-axis) against EFE total (y-axis). Three regions tell you what to do.

Stage 3 · the decision tool

The QSPM

The Quantitative Strategic Planning Matrix is the only technique designed to objectively rank competing strategies. It reuses Stage-1 weights and asks how attractive each strategy is, factor by factor.

  • AS (Attractiveness Score 1–4): 1 = not attractive … 4 = highly attractive.
  • TAS = weight × AS, computed row by row.
  • STAS = sum of TAS — the highest total wins.
  • Work row by row; never repeat the same AS across a row.
Decision rule

Higher Sum Total Attractiveness Score → the more attractive strategy to implement.

It turns "I think this is better" into a defensible number.

From strategy to recommendation

Estimating the cost of a recommendation

A recommendation is any strategy selected for implementation. The most important page of a case project lists each one with a 3-year dollar cost (or saving), summed to a total capital need.

Cost-per-unit

New plant ≈ $400/sq ft × 100K sq ft = $40M. New stores ≈ size × cost/sq ft × count.

Peer / 10-K lookup

Find what similar firms paid, or divide total PP&E by number of plants/stores for an average.

Acquire / divest

Acquisition ≈ segment's % share × target's total value. Divestiture frees that capital.

Spread & reason

Spread plant cost over ~2 yrs, stores over ~3. Avoid wild guesses — use "excellent estimates."

The human side · LO 6.10–6.11

Culture & politics in strategy choice

Culture

  • Success often rests on how much a strategy is supported by the firm's culture.
  • Strategies needing fewer cultural changes are more attractive — change is slow and costly.
  • Culture-strategy fit is critical in mergers, where two cultures collide.

Politics

  • All organizations are political; objective tools reduce the role of bias.
  • Successful strategists lead but don't dictate — they build coalitions and reserve vetoes for big issues.
  • A satisfactory result with a popular strategy often beats an optimal one nobody supports.
Six subjective threats personal biases · politics · prejudices · emotions · personalities · halo error — the very things the analytical framework is built to discipline.
